Section 1a — Foundations

How a Payment Moves

Every time a card is swiped, tapped, or entered online, a message travels through a chain of intermediaries in under 200 milliseconds.[1] Understanding this chain is the foundation of everything else in payments.

The transaction lifecycle has two distinct phases: authorization (does the bank approve this?) and settlement (does the money actually move?). These happen at very different timescales.

Batch settlement
At end of business, the merchant "batches out" — sending all authorized transactions for settlement. The acquirer sends these to the networks, which net the totals and initiate ACH transfers between banks. Funds arrive in the merchant's account minus all fees.

The cardholder can dispute the transaction with their bank. If the issuer finds in favor of the cardholder, the funds are "charged back" to the merchant. Excessive chargebacks can result in higher fees, fines, or account termination.
Key distinction

Authorization is about approval — it happens in milliseconds and just places a hold. Settlement is about actual money movement — it happens via the ACH network overnight. This is why a hotel can authorize $500 on check-in and settle a different amount on check-out.

Section 1b — Foundations

Gateway vs. Processor: The Core Distinction

These two terms are constantly conflated, even by people inside the industry. They solve different problems and operate at different layers of the stack.

Payment gateway
The data pipe

Takes sensitive card data from the merchant, encrypts it (or tokenizes it), and securely routes it to the processor. It's the digital equivalent of the POS terminal. It answers: "Did the card data get somewhere safely?"

Payment processor
The transaction engine

Formats and routes authorization requests to card networks, maintains bank relationships, manages interchange fees, and settles funds. It answers: "Is this card valid, and will the bank approve this transaction?"

In practice, many modern vendors have collapsed both functions into a single product. Stripe, Square, and Adyen all handle the gateway layer (encryption, tokenization, routing) and the processing layer (bank communication, settlement) in one integrated offering. You write to one API and never think about which layer you're touching.

However, the distinction matters when you're building a more complex integration. A standalone gateway like Authorize.net or NMI will require you to also choose a processor. A standalone processor like TSYS requires you to bring your own gateway. Traditional bank-owned processors almost never offer a compelling gateway product, which is why so many integrations mix and match.

When you'd keep them separate

Large enterprises often use a gateway orchestration layer (like Spreedly or Primer) that sits above multiple processors. This lets them route transactions to different processors based on geography, card type, cost, or uptime — a practice called payment orchestration. If Adyen is down in Europe, automatically route through Worldpay. If US debit is cheaper through one processor, use that one. The gateway handles the merchant-facing API; the processors compete behind it.

Section 1c — Foundations

The Fee Math: Where Every Cent Goes

On a $100 card transaction, the merchant doesn't receive $100. Here's exactly where the missing ~$2.50 goes and why.

Party Fee type Typical amount Notes
Issuing bank Interchange ~1.5–2.5% The largest fee. Varies by card type (rewards cards cost more), transaction type (card-present vs. CNP), merchant category code, and card network. Set by Visa/Mastercard,[3][4] not negotiable by merchants.
Card network Assessment / scheme fee ~0.10–0.15% Visa and Mastercard's revenue. Small per-transaction, but enormous at scale given billions of transactions. Includes per-transaction fees and a percentage.
Acquirer / processor Processor markup ~0.20–0.50% The acquirer's margin above interchange. Negotiated based on volume. Large enterprises can get this below 0.10%. Flat-rate pricing (Square, Stripe standard) bundles this into an all-in rate.
Merchant nets — ~$97.50 The remainder after all fees. High-risk industries (gambling, travel) pay significantly more. Regulated debit (Durbin-compliant) costs far less — as little as $0.05 + $0.21.

Interchange-plus vs. flat rate vs. subscription pricing

Flat-rate pricing (2.6% + $0.10 per swipe for Square,[5] 2.9% + $0.30 for Stripe's standard online rate[6]) is simple but expensive. The processor makes money on the spread between actual interchange and the flat rate you pay.

Interchange-plus (also called "cost-plus") passes actual interchange through to the merchant and adds a fixed processor markup (e.g., "interchange + 0.25% + $0.10"). Transparent, cheaper for high-volume merchants, but complex — your monthly fee varies because interchange rates vary.

Subscription / membership pricing (used by Stax, Payment Depot) charges a flat monthly fee and then passes interchange through at near-zero markup. Best for high-volume merchants with predictable transaction patterns.

The Durbin Amendment

For debit cards issued by banks with over $10B in assets, the Durbin Amendment (2010) caps interchange at $0.21 + 0.05% per transaction.[7] This is dramatically lower than credit card interchange and is why merchants prefer debit. Smaller issuing banks ("exempt" from Durbin) still charge higher debit interchange.

Section 2a — The Ecosystem

Vendor Landscape

The payments industry has six distinct vendor layers, each with different economics, risk profiles, and customer relationships.

The map below organizes every major vendor category from the foundational card networks — the rails that all transactions run on — down through full-stack PSPs, traditional processors, stand-alone gateways, the distribution and reseller layer, vertical-specific platforms, and finally the fraud, tokenization, and compliance tooling that sits across all of them. The key structural insight is that the layers are not a hierarchy of quality or sophistication: a traditional processor like Fiserv is not "below" Stripe in capability — they serve different markets and operate at different parts of the stack. Vendors in the PSP row have collapsed multiple layers into one product, which is why they often appear deceptively simple from the outside.

Section 2b — The Ecosystem

The Distribution Layer: ISOs, PayFacs, and ISVs

Beyond the core plumbing, there's an entire distribution and reseller layer most people don't know about. This is where most merchant relationships actually originate — and where much of the industry's margin lives.

ISO — Independent Sales Organization

An ISO is essentially a payments reseller. They sign a contract with an acquiring bank or processor, then go out and sign up merchants. They don't take on financial liability — the processor does. They earn a residual: a slice of the interchange spread on every transaction, paid monthly, for the life of the merchant relationship.

ISOs built the payments industry in the 1980s and 1990s. Large ISOs like North American Bancard and Priority Payments have tens of thousands of merchants under management. The residual model creates an asset that can be sold — an ISO's "merchant portfolio" can trade at 3–5x annual revenue.[12]

PayFac — Payment Facilitator

A PayFac holds a master MID (Merchant ID) and creates sub-merchant accounts beneath it. When a business signs up for Square or Toast, they're not getting their own MID — they're getting a sub-merchant ID under Square's umbrella. Square has already done the underwriting with the acquiring bank; it extends that to merchants in minutes.

PayFac economics

A PayFac earns the full processor margin (not just a residual), sets its own pricing, and owns the merchant relationship end-to-end. The trade-off: the PayFac absorbs fraud and chargeback risk on behalf of its sub-merchants. This is why Stripe holds funds for suspicious activity — the PayFac is protecting itself from sub-merchant fraud.

Becoming a PayFac the traditional way requires a direct contract with an acquiring bank, building fraud and underwriting systems, maintaining PCI compliance, and posting a large cash reserve (often $1M+).[13] PayFac-as-a-Service providers (Stripe Connect, Adyen for Platforms, Infinicept, PayFac-in-a-Box) let software companies get most of the economics without building the full infrastructure. They're sometimes called "managed PayFacs."

ISV — Independent Software Vendor

An ISV is a software company that has embedded payments into its product. Toast sells restaurant POS software but payments is a major part of its margin. Mindbody sells gym booking software; payments is embedded. ServiceTitan does HVAC/plumbing software; payments is embedded.

The ISV model is compelling because the software creates the merchant relationship first, then payments follow naturally. Switching costs become enormous once a business's operations are built around a platform — moving payment processors is hard; moving the system that runs every aspect of your business is harder.

Payments by Industry

Section 3a — By Industry

Brick-and-Mortar Retail

Physical retail is where modern payment infrastructure was built. Card-present transactions, EMV hardware, and deep inventory integration define the landscape. Payments requirements here are dominated by throughput, reliability, and increasingly, omnichannel unification.

The key dynamics in retail payments: card-present interchange is lower than card-not-present, meaning merchants actually benefit from customers paying in-store versus online. Post-2015, the US EMV liability shift[14] forced massive hardware upgrades, creating a replacement cycle that benefited POS vendors. Today, the pressing challenge is unifying in-store and online payment data — a customer buying online and returning in-store should be one customer record, not two.

Clover (Fiserv)

History: Clover was founded in 2010 as an independent startup building a next-generation POS system. Fiserv acquired it in 2012 for $160M[15] — at the time considered expensive — and used it to modernize its merchant-facing product. Clover runs on Android hardware and has an app marketplace that lets third-party developers extend its functionality, analogous to an App Store for POS.

Business model: Hardware sales (terminals from $49 to $600+) plus a mandatory Clover Payments processing account. Merchants pay flat-rate or interchange-plus depending on plan tier. Clover earns both hardware margin and the ongoing processing residual, making each merchant worth roughly $1,000–$3,000 in lifetime value. Fiserv distributes Clover through its ISO network and through banks, giving it a massive distribution advantage.

Strategy: Fiserv is pushing Clover upmarket toward multi-location and franchise operations. Clover Capital (merchant cash advances), payroll integrations, and loyalty tools are extending the ARPU per merchant. The long-term bet is becoming the full business operating system for SMB-to-mid-market retail.

NCR Voyix / Aloha

History: NCR (National Cash Register) invented the mechanical cash register in 1879 and has been a payments and POS hardware company ever since. By the 2000s it dominated enterprise retail and restaurant POS. In 2023, NCR split into two public companies: NCR Atleos (ATMs and banking) and NCR Voyix (POS and digital commerce).[16]

Business model: Enterprise software licensing, hardware, and managed services. NCR's customers are large retailers, grocery chains, and restaurants — clients with hundreds or thousands of lanes who need centralized management, complex inventory integration, and 24/7 support SLAs. Revenue is heavily weighted toward services and multi-year contracts.

Strategy: NCR Voyix is modernizing its legacy on-premise software to cloud-based platforms. Its Aloha restaurant POS and its retail platform are being rebuilt as SaaS products. The company faces significant competitive pressure from cloud-native challengers like Toast and Lightspeed but retains deep enterprise relationships that are expensive to dislodge.

Lightspeed Commerce

History: Founded in Montreal in 2005, Lightspeed built a cloud-native POS for specialty retailers (boutiques, outdoor shops, bike stores). It went public in 2019[17] and has grown aggressively through acquisition, buying ShopKeep, Vend, Ecwid, and NuOrder[18] to extend into restaurants, ecommerce, and B2B wholesale.

Business model: SaaS subscriptions plus embedded payments (Lightspeed Payments). Payments has become an increasingly dominant revenue line — Lightspeed charges a premium for its integrated payments and actively pushes merchants away from third-party processors. Its GTV (gross transaction value) has become the key operating metric investors watch.

Strategy: Unify retail, restaurant, ecommerce, and wholesale in a single platform. Lightspeed's ambition is to be the platform for complex, multi-channel specialty merchants who outgrow Square but don't need enterprise NCR. The acquisition of NuOrder adds B2B trade show and wholesale ordering — a differentiator no pure POS player has.

Section 3b — By Industry

Ecommerce and DTC

Ecommerce payments are defined by card-not-present risk, the checkout conversion imperative, and the rise of one-click wallets. Every additional millisecond of checkout friction and every unnecessary form field costs measurable revenue. The best ecommerce payment platforms are obsessed with authorization rates and conversion optimization, not just transaction processing.

The ecommerce payment stack has more layers than retail: a checkout UI, a gateway, fraud tools, 3DS authentication (when needed), the processor, and potentially a subscription billing engine. Full-stack PSPs like Stripe and Braintree have absorbed most of these layers, but large merchants often mix and match for cost or capability reasons.

Shopify Payments

History: Shopify launched in 2006 as a simple ecommerce platform. Shopify Payments launched in 2013,[19] powered by Stripe. In subsequent years Shopify built its own payment infrastructure and today operates as a full PayFac. It penalizes merchants who use competing payment processors (charging an additional 0.5–2% fee on non-Shopify Payments transactions[20]), effectively making Shopify Payments the default for most of its merchant base.

Business model: Shopify earns a take rate on every transaction processed through Shopify Payments — typically around 2.4–2.9% + $0.30 for standard merchants, with rates decreasing for higher-tier subscription plans. Shopify's "merchant solutions" revenue (which includes payments) has grown to be larger than its subscription revenue. Shopify also earns from Shopify Capital[21] (merchant cash advances), Shopify Balance (business banking), and Shopify Shipping.

Strategy: Shopify is building toward becoming the "operating system for commerce" — a platform where a merchant can run every aspect of their business. Offline expansion (Shopify POS) to capture omnichannel merchants, international expansion (local payment methods in 130+ countries), and enterprise upmarket push (Shopify Plus) are the three strategic vectors. The embedded finance stack (Capital, Balance, Credit) deepens lock-in and increases ARPU dramatically.

Stripe

History: Founded in 2010[22] by Patrick and John Collison, Stripe launched with a radical premise: a developer could integrate payments in seven lines of code rather than months of bank negotiations. It grew by word of mouth among developers and startups, then expanded upmarket. By 2023 Stripe had processed hundreds of billions in payments annually and was valued at $50B+ after a down round from its $95B peak.[23]

Business model: Stripe charges a flat rate (2.9% + $0.30 online) that hides its actual cost structure from small merchants, and moves to interchange-plus for large enterprise customers. Beyond core payments, Stripe has built a product suite including Radar (fraud), Billing (subscriptions), Connect (platforms), Atlas (company incorporation), Issuing (card issuing), Treasury (banking-as-a-service), and Tax. Each product either drives payment volume or earns its own revenue.

Strategy: Stripe is the most ambitious company in payments. Its stated goal is to grow the GDP of the internet — an aspiration that frames every product as infrastructure for the businesses that use it. The near-term strategy is moving further upmarket (landing more Amazons and Fords), expanding in emerging markets (India, Southeast Asia, Africa), and deepening the financial services stack so that startups can use Stripe as their only financial provider from incorporation through IPO.

Braintree (PayPal)

History: Braintree was founded in 2007 and acquired by PayPal in 2013 for $800M[24] — a deal that also brought Venmo into the PayPal family. For years Braintree was PayPal's developer-facing gateway while PayPal remained the consumer-facing wallet. Under a single parent, the two products competed awkwardly with each other.

Business model: Braintree offers interchange-plus pricing for larger merchants and flat-rate for smaller ones. The unique advantage is the PayPal network — when a Braintree-powered checkout includes the PayPal button, conversion rates measurably increase because shoppers trust and already have PayPal accounts. This network effect is Braintree's primary differentiator against Stripe.

Strategy: PayPal has struggled with strategic clarity for Braintree. The company has been cutting processor fees to win large merchants (sometimes at near-zero margin on the acquiring side) in hopes of driving PayPal wallet attach. The real long-term bet is on "PayPal Everywhere" — an advertising and commerce network built on PayPal's 400M+ consumer accounts, where merchants pay for access to PayPal's audience rather than just for payment processing.

Section 3c — By Industry

Restaurants

Restaurant payments are uniquely complex: tip adjustments require auth and settle at different amounts, open tabs can last hours, split checks are routine, and the kitchen, the front of house, and the online ordering system all need to stay synchronized. The pandemic permanently accelerated QR code ordering and contactless payments, reshaping the tech stack of every full-service restaurant.

The dominant dynamic in restaurant payments is vertical integration: the winning vendors have moved far beyond payment processing to own scheduling, payroll, inventory, online ordering, loyalty, and even supplier payments. Payments are the anchor, but the platform is the moat.

Toast

History: Toast was founded in Boston in 2011, initially as a generic tablet-based POS. It pivoted specifically to restaurants around 2013 and never looked back. It went public in September 2021 at a $20B valuation,[25] one of the largest restaurant-tech IPOs ever. Toast's rise coincided with the iPad POS wave but distinguished itself by building restaurant-specific software rather than adapting a generic platform.

Business model: Toast earns from four streams: SaaS subscriptions (POS software, payroll, scheduling), hardware (terminals, kiosks, kitchen display systems), payments (a take rate on every transaction processed through Toast Payments — required for most customers), and financial services (Toast Capital, working capital loans underwritten by payment history). Payments is estimated to represent roughly 75–80% of Toast's revenue. The company charges a processing fee slightly above market rates but bundles it with software discounts to make the all-in cost competitive.

Strategy: Toast is executing a "land and expand" strategy within each restaurant relationship. The initial sale is POS software; subsequent products are payroll (Toast Payroll), scheduling (Toast Scheduling), online ordering (Toast Online Ordering), loyalty (Toast Loyalty), and supplier payments (Toast Invoicing). Each new product increases ARPU and switching cost. Internationally, Toast has begun expansion into Canada, Ireland, and the UK. The long-term bet is becoming the financial operating system for the restaurant industry.

Square for Restaurants

History: Square launched its restaurant-specific POS in 2018 after years of powering café-style food service businesses on its generic POS. It was designed to compete with Toast in fast-casual and quick-service restaurants, though it has never matched Toast in full-service.

Business model: Same as Square's core model — flat-rate processing (2.6% + $0.10 for card-present), hardware sales, and tiered SaaS subscriptions for restaurant features. Square for Restaurants integrates with the broader Square ecosystem (payroll, banking, marketing), making the total platform compelling for operators who want one vendor.

Strategy: Square positions restaurants as part of a broader SMB ecosystem play rather than the sole focus. Its advantage is breadth and simplicity; its weakness is depth versus Toast. Square's strategy leans on its banking and lending products (Square Banking, Square Loans) to add financial services revenue per restaurant customer. Toast's deep restaurant verticalization makes it hard to displace for full-service operators.

SpotOn

History: SpotOn was founded in 2017 and initially focused on merchant software and loyalty tools rather than POS. It raised aggressively and pivoted into a full restaurant and retail technology platform by 2019. Backed by $300M+ in venture funding,[26] it competed directly with Toast on POS and payments, often winning on price and customer service.

Business model: Integrated POS, payments, and marketing tools. SpotOn's processing is priced competitively to undercut Toast, and it earns on software subscriptions. Unlike Toast's high-volume growth strategy, SpotOn has positioned itself on customer service and flexibility — it's more willing to accommodate custom configurations and local support arrangements.

Strategy: SpotOn targets the segment of restaurant operators who feel underserved by Toast's standardized approach and Square's simplicity. Its differentiator is service quality and configurability. Long-term, SpotOn is building out enterprise capabilities for franchise and chain operators, a market Toast already dominates.

Section 3d — By Industry

Small and Medium Businesses

SMB payments were transformed by Square in 2009. Before Square, accepting card payments required a bank relationship, a lease on a terminal, and months of paperwork. Square made it a 10-minute signup with a $0 reader. That model — instant onboarding, flat-rate pricing, and a phone-as-terminal — became the template the entire industry copied.

SMB is defined less by transaction size and more by operational complexity. An SMB owner is often also the accountant, marketer, and HR department. Payment solutions that require a dedicated IT team or a specialist to configure are a non-starter. The trade-off: simplicity costs money. Flat-rate pricing is materially more expensive than interchange-plus at scale.

Square (Block)

History: Jack Dorsey and Jim McKelvey founded Square in 2009[27] after McKelvey lost a sale because he couldn't accept credit cards. The iconic white card reader — a small square dongle that plugged into a headphone jack — became one of the most recognized product designs of the 2010s. Square went public in 2015 and rebranded to Block in 2021[28] to reflect its expansion beyond SMB payments into Bitcoin, music, and banking.

Business model: Square earns from hardware, payment processing (2.6% + $0.10 card-present, 2.9% + $0.30 online), SaaS subscriptions (Square for Restaurants, Square for Retail, Appointments, etc.), Square Banking (business checking accounts), Square Loans (merchant cash advances), and Cash App (consumer P2P and investing). Payments processing is still the largest revenue driver, but the financial services businesses are growing faster.

Strategy: Block's strategy is to build interconnected ecosystems for merchants (Square) and consumers (Cash App) and eventually connect them. The long-term vision is a closed-loop financial network where small businesses pay their employees in Cash App, employees spend via Cash App Card, and that spend flows back through Square merchants. Bitcoin is a speculative long-term bet on an alternative settlement rail. Near-term, Square is moving upmarket — adding features for mid-market multi-location operators who previously needed a more expensive platform.

Stripe (SMB tier)

History: Though Stripe is primarily known as a developer tool for sophisticated businesses, its flat-rate pricing tier (no monthly fees, 2.9% + $0.30) has attracted many SMBs, especially those with an online component. Stripe Terminal extended its reach to card-present transactions.

Business model: At the SMB tier, Stripe is running a high-margin flat-rate processing business. The goal is to grow SMBs into the enterprise customers where Stripe's real margin is. A startup that uses Stripe at $10K/month in volume often stays on Stripe when it reaches $10M/month — at which point Stripe negotiates custom pricing and earns from the full product suite.

Strategy: Stripe doesn't actively market to SMBs the way Square does. SMBs find Stripe through developer communities, e-commerce platforms, and word of mouth. Stripe's SMB strategy is more about removing barriers (no contracts, no monthly fees, instant setup) than about acquiring SMBs as a segment. The real bet is that today's garage startup is tomorrow's high-growth enterprise customer.

Helcim

History: Founded in Canada in 2006, Helcim is one of the most respected independent payment processors for SMBs that have outgrown flat-rate pricing. It offers interchange-plus pricing transparently and has built a reputation for honest pricing and customer service — a stark contrast to the hidden-fee practices common in traditional ISO channels.

Business model: Interchange-plus processing plus a free POS, invoicing, and payment page software suite. Helcim's model is counter-intuitive: it charges less per transaction than competitors and makes money on volume by attracting merchants with higher processing needs who are tired of overpaying on flat-rate plans. No contracts and no monthly minimums.

Strategy: Helcim is playing the long game of being the most trusted processor for cost-conscious SMBs. It has avoided venture capital and grown organically. The strategy is not to compete on features or embedded finance — it's to be the processor that the business community trusts, particularly for independent retailers, service businesses, and professionals.

Section 3e — By Industry

Enterprise

Enterprise payments are defined by cost optimization at scale, global acquiring, and deep system integration. When a company processes $1B in annual card volume, a one-basis-point improvement in net processing cost is worth $100,000 per year. This makes pricing negotiations, interchange optimization, and network fee management top priorities. The best enterprise payment platforms are also sophisticated financial engineering platforms.

Enterprise requirements differ from SMB in almost every dimension: months-long RFPs replace instant signup, direct bank relationships replace PayFac sub-merchant accounts, Level 2/3 data submission optimizes B2B card interchange, and global acquiring replaces single-country processing. The competitive dynamics are dominated by Adyen, FIS/Worldpay, and Fiserv, with Stripe increasingly winning enterprise accounts.

Adyen

History: Founded in Amsterdam in 2006 by Pieter van der Does and Arnout Schuijff, who previously led payments at Bibit (acquired by Royal Bank of Scotland). Adyen was built from scratch as a direct acquirer — meaning it holds acquiring licenses in every major market rather than reselling another bank's acquiring. This gave it significantly lower per-transaction costs and more control. It went public in Amsterdam in 2018 at a €7B valuation and reached €50B+ at peak.[29]

Business model: Adyen charges a processing fee (a small fixed amount per transaction) plus a percentage markup above interchange. Because it's a direct acquirer, its cost structure is lower than PSPs that sit atop another acquirer. The model is deliberately unprofitable at small volumes — Adyen does not want SMB customers — and extremely profitable at high volumes. Major clients include McDonald's, Uber, Microsoft, Spotify, eBay, and H&M.

Strategy: Adyen is executing a "unified commerce" strategy: one platform for online, in-store, and in-app payments, with one data view across all channels. This is compelling for global retailers who previously had separate processors for their website and their stores. Adyen is also expanding into banking services (Adyen for Platforms, Adyen Banking as a Service) to capture more of the financial services stack. The North America mid-market — historically underserved by Adyen's enterprise-minimum focus — is a current growth priority.

FIS / Worldpay

History: FIS (Fidelity National Information Services) was founded in 1968 as a bank technology company. Worldpay has a more complicated lineage: it was carved out of RBS (Royal Bank of Scotland) in 2010, went public, then merged with Vantiv in 2018 to form a combined Worldpay that FIS then acquired for $43B in 2019[30] — one of the largest payments deals ever. FIS then divested Worldpay back to private equity (GTCR) in 2023 for approximately $18.5B[31], recognizing the deal had been overpriced. The two companies now operate independently again.

Business model: FIS is primarily an issuer processor (the software banks use to run their card programs) and banking technology company. Worldpay is a merchant acquirer and payment processor focused on large enterprise merchants, ecommerce platforms, and global payments. Worldpay processes roughly $2.2T in annual payment volume.[32]

Strategy: Under private equity ownership, Worldpay is focused on margin expansion and operational efficiency rather than growth investment. FIS is rationalizing its product portfolio after years of acquisition-led complexity. Both companies face the challenge of modernizing legacy infrastructure that handles critical banking and payments functions for thousands of institutions while avoiding disruption.

Fiserv (enterprise tier)

History: Fiserv was founded in 1984 as a bank data processing company. Its 2019 acquisition of First Data for $22B[33] created the largest merchant acquirer in the US by merchant count. First Data's Clover POS system and its STAR debit network gave Fiserv assets that extended beyond processing into merchant-facing software and the debit infrastructure layer.

Business model: Fiserv earns from financial institution technology (core banking software, card management) and from merchant acquiring (payment processing, POS hardware through Clover). Enterprise merchants use Fiserv's Carat platform — a unified commerce gateway that handles omnichannel transactions. Large bank clients use Fiserv to run their entire card programs.

Strategy: Fiserv is bet on the convergence of banking and commerce. Its unique position — serving both the banks that issue cards and the merchants that accept them — gives it data and relationship advantages no pure-play processor has. Clover is being pushed upmarket toward franchise and multi-location enterprise operators. Carat is the enterprise gateway that competes with Cybersource and Adyen for large omnichannel merchants.

The PayFac Model: A Deeper Look

The payment facilitator model has become the dominant structure for software-led payments. Understanding its mechanics — and its risks — is essential for anyone building in this space.

How sub-merchant onboarding works

When a merchant signs up under a PayFac, they go through a streamlined KYC (Know Your Customer) process — typically business name, EIN, bank account, and a few owner details. The PayFac has pre-built risk models that can approve most businesses in seconds. This compares to 1–4 weeks for a traditional ISO/bank merchant onboarding.

The PayFac assigns the sub-merchant a sub-MID and sets transaction limits (e.g., max $10,000 per transaction) until the business is more thoroughly vetted. Higher-risk or higher-volume businesses may require additional documentation.

Risk and liability: the PayFac's burden

The acquiring bank sees only the PayFac as the merchant. All chargebacks and fraud losses from any sub-merchant are the PayFac's problem. PayFacs handle this through a combination of:

  • →Rolling reserves — holding 5–10% of processing volume in an escrow account for 90+ days as a buffer against delayed chargebacks.
  • →Transaction monitoring — real-time ML models flagging anomalous transaction patterns, velocity checks, and suspicious merchant behavior.
  • →MCC restrictions — refusing to board merchants in high-risk categories (firearms, cannabis, adult content, cryptocurrency) entirely.
  • →Fund holds — delaying payouts when unusual activity is detected. This is the mechanism behind the infamous "Stripe held my funds" complaints.

PayFac-as-a-Service: the managed model

Building a true PayFac requires a direct acquiring bank relationship, significant capital reserves, in-house fraud and compliance teams, and PCI Level 1 certification. Most software companies use a managed PayFac model instead:

Section 4b — Advanced Topics

Embedded Finance: Beyond Payments

Embedded finance is the broader movement where financial services — payments, lending, banking, insurance — get distributed through non-financial software products. Payments is the foundation; it's rarely where it ends. The ISVs that have executed this strategy most successfully have transformed from software companies into financial services companies with a software distribution advantage.

This dynamic is best captured by the pattern across every successful vertical SaaS company that has pursued it: once payroll, payments, and capital all flow through one platform, the cost of switching approaches infinity. The software is the trojan horse; financial services are the castle.

Why embedded finance has become the dominant trend

Three forces converged in the 2010s to make embedded finance inevitable.

The first was the collapse of the compliance and licensing cost floor. Before 2012, becoming a payments company required bank relationships, PCI Level 1 certification, significant capital reserves, and a team of compliance specialists. Stripe, then Adyen for Platforms, then a wave of PayFac-as-a-Service providers (Infinicept, Payrix, Stripe Connect) packaged all of that complexity into APIs. The cost to embed payments in a software product dropped from $5M and 18 months to $50K and 3 months. When the barrier dropped, every software company asked: why aren't we doing this?

The second was the realization that payments data is the most valuable data asset a software company can have. If you know what a business is selling, to whom, at what price, with what margins, and with what seasonality — because all of that flows through your payment processing — you can underwrite loans more accurately than any bank, offer insurance at better rates than any insurer, and build loyalty and marketing tools with higher ROI than any ad platform. Payment data isn't just revenue; it's an intelligence moat.

The third was the softwareization of every industry vertical. In the 2010s, every previously paper-based industry got a software platform — restaurants got Toast, HVAC companies got ServiceTitan, gym owners got Mindbody, construction companies got Procore. Each of these platforms owned the merchant relationship before they added payments. When they did add payments, adoption was nearly frictionless — the merchant was already using the software daily, already trusted the vendor, and would experience immediate workflow benefits from having payments integrated. Merchant acquisition cost was effectively zero because the software had already acquired the relationship.

The unit economics advantage

A vertical SaaS company earns perhaps $200/year per customer on software alone. Add embedded payments at 40bps above cost on $500K of annual processing volume, and the same customer is worth $2,200/year — an 11× improvement in ARPU. Add a merchant cash advance at $10K once every 18 months, and the LTV grows further. The software is the acquisition channel; financial services are the monetization engine.

The progression of embedded finance

Most platforms start with payment acceptance. From there, the natural progression is:

1. Payment acceptance
Accept cards, ACH, and wallets through the platform. Earn payment revenue. (Toast, Mindbody, ServiceTitan all started here.)
2. Payouts and disbursements
Send money out — pay contractors, tip-out employees, distribute marketplace earnings. Earns interchange on outbound cards or fees on ACH/instant payouts.
3. Stored value / banking
Offer a business bank account or wallet. Funds stay on-platform longer. Earn float income and interchange on debit card spend. (Square Banking, Shopify Balance.)
4. Capital / lending
Offer merchant cash advances or revenue-based loans using payment history as underwriting. Repaid as a percentage of daily sales. (Square Loans, Shopify Capital, Toast Capital, Stripe Capital.)
5. Insurance and cards
Offer business insurance (workers' comp, general liability) or issue corporate cards. Earns premium or interchange. Creates additional switching costs.

The following companies represent the current high-water mark for embedded finance execution. Each started as a software company for a specific industry, layered in payments, and has continued expanding the financial services stack.

Toast — Restaurants

Toast is the clearest case study in embedded finance done right. Founded in 2011, it reached roughly $1.1B in revenue by 2023[34] — with approximately 85% of that coming from financial technology (payments, capital, banking) rather than software subscriptions.[34] Its payments take rate is modestly above the market, but merchants accept it because the workflow integration is worth more than the few basis points they'd save by switching processors. Toast Capital offers working capital loans at rates that are high by bank standards but fast and frictionless by operator standards — funds often arrive the next day. The future path is international expansion and more deeply embedding into the restaurant supply chain: supplier payments, food cost management, and potentially insurance for restaurant-specific risks (food spoilage, equipment breakdown).

Shopify's embedded finance arc is the most complete of any vertical SaaS company. Shopify Payments launched in 2013; Shopify Capital[35] (merchant cash advances) launched in 2016 and has since deployed billions in loans; Shopify Balance[36] (business banking) launched in 2020; Shopify Credit (a business credit card) launched in 2023.[37] The company has effectively become a full-service financial institution for online merchants — without a banking license. The enforcement mechanism is subtle but powerful: Shopify charges additional processing fees (0.5–2%) on merchants who use non-Shopify Payments processors, creating a financial penalty for leaving the platform. Its strategy for the future involves expanding financial services internationally and moving into B2B payments (business-to-business commerce is 10× larger than consumer ecommerce by volume).

ServiceTitan is less famous than Toast or Shopify but arguably has the most elegant embedded finance execution for a high-value service business. Founded in 2012, it serves HVAC, plumbing, electrical, and other home service contractors. These businesses are cash-flow volatile — revenue spikes in summer and winter, equipment purchases are lumpy, and technicians need to collect payment at the job site. ServiceTitan Payments handles job-site card collection. ServiceTitan Capital offers working capital. ServiceTitan has become the operating system for an industry that was previously running on whiteboards and paper invoices. The company went public in 2024 at a valuation north of $5B.[38] Its future strategy involves international expansion and moving into adjacent trades (pest control, landscaping, pool service).

Founded in 2001 (well before the modern ISV-payments era), Mindbody built scheduling and business management software for fitness studios, yoga studios, salons, and spas. It was acquired by Vista Equity Partners in 2019[39] and has since aggressively expanded its payments and financial services offering. Because fitness is a subscription-heavy industry, recurring billing quality (authorization rates, card-updater performance, dunning) is a competitive differentiator. The company has also built a consumer app[39] (the Mindbody consumer marketplace) that creates demand for the studios using its software — an unusual distribution flywheel where the software company also drives customer acquisition for its merchant base.

Procore built the construction management software platform and has been systematically adding financial layers: Procore Pay enables payments between project participants, reducing the cash flow crunch that plagues subcontractors (who often wait 60–90 days to be paid). Procore's payments play is more about enabling faster B2B payments across complex construction payment chains than about consumer-style card processing. If Procore succeeds in becoming the financial clearing house for construction project payments, it captures a share of trillions in annual construction spend.

What PAR Is — and Isn't

PAR is a 29-character alphanumeric identifier, standardized by EMVCo[41] (the consortium behind EMV chip standards, run jointly by Amex, Discover, JCB, Mastercard, UnionPay, and Visa[41]). It is:

  • ✓ Stable for the lifetime of the underlying PAN (reissued only when the PAN changes)
  • ✓ A reference number — cannot be used to initiate a transaction or derive the PAN
  • ✓ The same value returned for any token derived from the same PAN (physical card, Apple Pay DPAN, network token)
  • ✗ Not a global universal ID — Visa generates a PAR, Mastercard generates a separate PAR for the same card. They are not interoperable across networks.
  • ✗ Not considered non-PII — linkable to a real person. Merchants must treat it with PII-equivalent care under GDPR and CCPA.

How PAR is generated

PAR generation is the responsibility of the card network, in coordination with the issuing bank. The specific mechanism differs slightly by network — Visa generates PAR through its Token Service Provider infrastructure; Mastercard generates it through MDES — but the conceptual model is identical across both.

PAR is not derived from the PAN algorithmically. It is a separately assigned identifier that the network creates and stores in a lookup table alongside the PAN. This means there is no way to compute a PAR from a card number, and no way to reverse a PAR back to a PAN — which is precisely what makes it safe to share with merchants.

  1. 01 Card issuance. When a bank issues a new physical card, it registers the PAN with the card network. The network generates a PAR for that PAN and stores it in a central lookup table. This happens before any transaction ever occurs.
  2. 02 Token provisioning (wallets). When the cardholder adds the card to Apple Pay, Google Pay, or Samsung Pay, the mobile device calls the network's Token Service Provider (Visa VTS[42] or Mastercard MDES[43]). The TSP creates a Device PAN (DPAN) specific to that device and links it to the existing PAR for the underlying PAN. Multiple DPANs — one per device — all share the same PAR.
  3. 03 Network token provisioning (merchants). When a merchant or PSP requests a network token to replace a stored PAN in their vault (using Visa Token Service or Mastercard MDES APIs), the network creates a Merchant Token linked to the same PAR. A merchant with 500,000 stored cards may have 500,000 network tokens, each pointing to the same PAR as the original physical card.
  4. 04 Authorization response. When any transaction is authorized — whether from a physical card, a DPAN, or a network token — the PAR is included in the authorization response message that travels back through the processing chain. For Visa, PAR appears in the Data Record field of the auth response. For Mastercard, it appears in Data Element 48, Sub-Element 55.
  5. 05 Card replacement. When a card is reissued due to expiry or compromise, the issuer assigns a new PAN — but the network preserves the original PAR for that cardholder account. This is the PAR's most powerful property: even when the card number changes, PAR-based identity linking continues uninterrupted.

Which parties in the ecosystem see PAR — and at what point

PAR travels in the authorization response, which means every party that processes that response message has the opportunity to receive and store it. Not all of them do — support depends on software version, configuration, and whether the party has implemented the relevant field parsing. Here is the full visibility chain:

Card network (Visa / Mastercard)
The originating authority. The network generates PAR, stores the PAN→PAR mapping in its Token Vault, and injects PAR into every authorization response it routes. Has complete visibility across all PAR values for all cards on its network.
Issuing bank
Receives PAR from the network and stores it in its core banking system alongside the PAN. Uses it for dispute resolution (matching a disputed transaction to the correct account), cardholder analytics, and statement enrichment. The issuer also originates the PAR assignment request when a new card is created.
Acquiring bank / processor
Receives PAR in the auth response from the network and can pass it downstream in the settlement file and transaction reporting. Large acquirers like Adyen, Stripe, Fiserv, and Chase Paymentech all support PAR in their API responses and transaction data exports. Whether PAR appears in the data a merchant receives depends entirely on the acquirer's implementation.
Payment gateway
The gateway sits between the merchant and the processor and may surface PAR in its transaction records, webhooks, and API responses. Stripe, Adyen, and Braintree all include PAR in their payment intent/charge objects when the underlying network returns it. Standalone gateways like Authorize.net and NMI also expose PAR in their reporting APIs, though merchant adoption of this field is inconsistent. A merchant that has not explicitly built PAR storage logic will simply receive and discard it.
Merchant (direct receipt)
The terminal point of PAR distribution. The merchant receives PAR in the gateway/processor API response or in the batch settlement file, if their acquirer passes it through. Merchants that store PAR alongside their internal customer record can link payment instrument changes to the same customer identity — the core use case PAR was designed for. Most merchants do not yet store PAR, either because their acquirer doesn't expose it or because they haven't built the integration.
Fraud / analytics platforms (third-party)
Fraud vendors like Kount, Sift, and Forter receive transaction data from their merchant clients — including PAR where available. Cross-merchant PAR sharing (with cardholder consent or under a consortium agreement) is the frontier use case: if a cardholder defrauds Merchant A using Token X, and that Token X carries PAR Y, a fraud network can flag PAR Y across all member merchants even if the fraudster rotates to a new token. This requires careful privacy governance and explicit network agreements to implement legally.
Important caveat: PAR support is not universal

The card networks began including PAR in auth responses in 2019 (Visa) and 2020 (Mastercard).[41] However, software support up and down the chain — processors, gateways, merchant systems — is still being rolled out. A merchant using an older payment integration may receive transactions where PAR is simply absent. As of 2024–2025, major modern PSPs (Stripe, Adyen, Braintree) reliably return PAR; legacy standalone gateways and regional processors vary significantly.

Who uses PAR and how — with real-world examples

🎯
Merchants — loyalty and recognition
  • Link a customer's Apple Pay purchase to their physical card in the loyalty database
  • Award points regardless of which payment method was used
  • Recognize a returning customer even if they got a new card (new PAN, same PAR)
  • Build cross-channel customer profiles (in-store + online unified)
Real-world example

Starbucks rewards are tied to the payment card registered in the app. Before PAR, a customer who added their Visa to Apple Pay and paid with their phone at the register would not have their stars credited — the DPAN didn't match the stored PAN. With PAR, Starbucks can look up the PAR returned in the transaction, match it to the stored PAR from enrollment, and credit the reward regardless of which token form factor was used. The same logic applies when a card is reissued: the customer gets a new card number, but PAR stays the same, so the loyalty account link survives without the customer needing to re-add their card.

🔄
Subscription platforms
  • Detect when a failed recurring payment card has been replaced
  • Match the new network token to the old one via shared PAR
  • Improve account updater hit rates (automatic card number refresh)
  • Reduce involuntary churn from card-not-updated failures
Real-world example

A Netflix subscriber's Visa card expires in December. Netflix's Account Updater service queries Visa for an updated PAN, but if the bank issued a new card number (not just a new expiry), the PAN changes and the Account Updater may not catch it immediately. Netflix's next billing attempt declines. Without PAR, this becomes an involuntary churn event requiring the customer to manually re-enter their card. With PAR, Netflix can detect that the new card presented in any subsequent transaction — or obtained through a recovery flow — carries the same PAR as the expired card, confirm it's the same account, and link the subscription without requiring customer action. This is why PAR reduces passive churn for subscription businesses with large card portfolios.

🛡️
Fraud and risk systems
  • Link suspicious behavior across multiple payment tokens to one person
  • Detect when a bad actor uses a new token to circumvent a PAN-based block
  • Build cross-merchant velocity checks across card instruments
  • Improve fraud model accuracy with richer behavioral profiles
Real-world example

A fraudster uses a stolen physical card (PAN ending 1234) to make a $300 purchase at an online retailer. The retailer's fraud system flags and blocks PAN 1234. The fraudster then provisions the same stolen card to Google Pay to get a new DPAN (ending 9876) and attempts another transaction at the same merchant. Without PAR, the fraud system sees a completely new card and may not catch the repeated fraud attempt. With PAR, the system recognizes that the DPAN and the blocked PAN share the same PAR — meaning same account, same device, same person — and can apply the existing block immediately, regardless of which token was used. Fraud platforms that operate across multiple merchants can extend this: if PAR Y is flagged at Merchant A, all consortium members can be notified to watch for PAR Y, even if the fraudster switches tokens at Merchant B.

📊
Issuers and networks
  • Unify cardholder transaction history across tokens for portfolio analytics
  • Improve dispute resolution across token changes
  • Enable cross-token spend analysis without exposing PAN
  • Support regulatory reporting with a stable cardholder reference
Real-world example

A Chase cardholder files a dispute claiming they never authorized a $120 transaction at an online merchant. The transaction was processed using the card's Apple Pay DPAN — a different token than the physical card's PAN. In the old model, Chase's dispute team would need to manually correlate the DPAN (returned in the chargeback) with the underlying PAN in their card management system to confirm the card belongs to this customer. With PAR indexed alongside both the PAN and the DPAN in Chase's core system, the dispute team can instantly confirm account ownership and retrieve the full transaction history regardless of which token was used for each purchase. This also prevents a class of friendly fraud where cardholders dispute transactions made on a wallet token, claiming the transaction "wasn't on their card" — the PAR link makes that defense untenable.

Privacy considerations

PAR is not regulated the same as a PAN — there is no "PAR security standard" equivalent to PCI-DSS — but it is unambiguously linkable to a real person. Under GDPR (EU), CCPA (California), and most modern privacy frameworks, PAR qualifies as personal data or personal information. Merchants storing PAR must include it in their data inventory, honor deletion requests, and disclose it in privacy notices. Processors storing PAR at scale should treat it as PII-equivalent in their data governance programs.

Glossary

Key terms used throughout this guide.

3DS / 3D Secure
An authentication protocol (versions 1.0 and 2.x) developed by EMVCo that adds a cardholder verification step to card-not-present transactions. Branded as "Verified by Visa," "Mastercard Identity Check," and "American Express SafeKey." When triggered, the issuing bank authenticates the cardholder — via biometric, one-time passcode, or silent risk assessment — before the transaction is approved. The US electronic bank-to-bank network used for direct debits and credits. Slower and cheaper than card rails. Commonly used for payroll, bill pay, and B2B payments. Same-day ACH is increasingly common.
API (Application Programming Interface)
A defined contract that allows two software systems to communicate. In payments, APIs are how merchants connect to gateways and processors. Modern PSPs like Stripe and Braintree are "API-first" — the entire payment experience is configured through code rather than hardware or manual setup.
ARPU (Average Revenue Per User)
A key SaaS and platform metric measuring average revenue generated per customer per period. In the context of embedded finance, ARPU is the primary reason ISVs add payments: a $200/year software customer generating $500K in annual payment volume at 40bps above cost becomes a $2,200/year customer — an 11× ARPU improvement.
AVS (Address Verification System)
A fraud-prevention check used in card-not-present transactions where the billing address entered by the cardholder is compared against the address on file at the issuing bank. AVS mismatches are a fraud signal. Not a definitive fraud indicator — cardholders often have multiple addresses on file — but a standard layer of CNP verification alongside CVV.
Acquiring bank / acquirer
The financial institution that holds the merchant's account and accepts card transactions on their behalf. The acquirer is responsible for settling funds to the merchant after receiving them from the card networks.
Authorization
The real-time process by which the issuing bank approves or declines a transaction and places a hold on the cardholder's funds. Distinct from settlement (actual money movement).
BIN (Bank Identification Number)
The first 6–8 digits of a card number that identify the issuing bank and card type. Used by processors to route transactions to the correct network.
Card-present (CP)
Transactions where the physical card is present at the point of sale — swiped (magnetic stripe), dipped (EMV chip), or tapped (NFC/contactless). Because the card and cardholder are physically there, fraud risk is substantially lower than card-not-present. Card-present transactions attract lower interchange rates as a result. EMV chip transactions are the most secure form of card-present, as the chip generates a unique cryptogram per transaction that cannot be replayed by fraudsters.
Card-not-present (CNP)
Transactions where the physical card is not presented — online payments, phone orders, mail-order/telephone-order (MOTO), and in-app purchases. Because the merchant cannot verify the cardholder is holding the card, fraud risk is higher, and interchange rates reflect that. CNP transactions require additional verification measures: CVV/CVC checks, AVS (Address Verification System), and increasingly 3D Secure (Verified by Visa / Mastercard Identity Check) to authenticate the cardholder before the transaction is approved.
BOPIS (Buy Online, Pick Up In Store)
A fulfillment model where a customer purchases online (card-not-present) but collects the order in a physical store. From a payments perspective, BOPIS transactions are CNP at checkout but may involve an in-store card-present interaction for returns or age verification. A key driver of omnichannel complexity for retail payment systems.
CVV / CVC (Card Verification Value / Card Verification Code)
The 3- or 4-digit security code printed on a payment card (not embossed, not stored on the magnetic stripe) that is used to verify card-not-present transactions. Visa calls it CVV2; Mastercard calls it CVC2; Amex uses a 4-digit CID on the card front. Under PCI-DSS, merchants are prohibited from storing CVV/CVC after authorization. Its presence in a CNP transaction indicates the person submitting payment has physical access to the card.
Chargeback
A reversal of a transaction initiated by the cardholder's bank, typically due to a dispute. The merchant must return funds and may incur a chargeback fee ($15–$100). Excessive chargebacks result in higher processing fees or account termination.
DPAN (Device Primary Account Number)
A tokenized card number provisioned to a specific device (e.g., iPhone for Apple Pay). Cannot be used to derive the underlying PAN. Unique per device per card.
Dunning
The process of automatically retrying failed recurring payments and communicating with customers about declined cards. Critical for subscription businesses to reduce involuntary churn.
DTC (Direct-to-Consumer)
A business model where a brand sells directly to end customers, bypassing traditional retail distribution. In payment terms, DTC brands operate primarily in card-not-present ecommerce environments and depend heavily on checkout conversion optimization, stored card tokenization, and subscription billing.
EMV (Europay, Mastercard, Visa)
The global standard for chip-based payment cards. EMV chips generate a unique cryptogram per transaction, making card-present fraud dramatically harder than with magnetic stripes.
FPAN (Funding PAN / Full PAN)
The actual underlying card number — the PAN stored at the issuer — as distinguished from a token (DPAN or network token). When tokenization documentation refers to "FPAN→" in a token field, it means the system is mapping from a full card number to its tokenized surrogate. The FPAN itself is never transmitted in a properly tokenized payment flow.
GTV (Gross Transaction Value)
The total monetary value of all transactions processed through a platform in a given period, before any fees are deducted. GTV (also called GPV — Gross Payment Volume — by some companies) is a primary growth metric for payment platforms and ISVs because it determines the absolute size of the revenue opportunity. A platform earning 40bps on $1B of GTV earns $4M in net payment revenue.
Interchange
The fee paid by the merchant's acquiring bank to the cardholder's issuing bank on every card transaction. It is the largest single component of a merchant's processing cost — typically 1.5–2.5% of the transaction amount for credit cards. Interchange rates are set by Visa and Mastercard (not the processor), published in detailed rate tables, and vary based on: card type (debit vs. credit; standard vs. rewards vs. corporate), transaction method (card-present vs. card-not-present), merchant category code (MCC), and transaction size. Merchants cannot directly negotiate interchange — it is non-negotiable and paid regardless of which processor is used. The Durbin Amendment (2010) caps debit interchange for large US banks at $0.21 + 0.05% per transaction, dramatically below credit card rates.
Interchange-plus pricing
A pricing model (also called "cost-plus") where the merchant pays actual interchange — whatever Visa or Mastercard set for each specific transaction — plus a fixed markup charged by the processor (e.g., "interchange + 0.25% + $0.10 per transaction"). This is the most transparent pricing model: the processor's margin is explicit and stable, and the merchant can see exactly what interchange category each transaction falls into. It is typically cheaper than flat-rate pricing for high-volume merchants, but harder to budget for because the effective rate fluctuates as card mix changes (a month with more premium rewards cards costs more than a month with more debit). Large and mid-market merchants almost universally use interchange-plus; flat-rate pricing (where the processor bundles interchange into a single all-in rate) is more common at the SMB level where simplicity outweighs cost optimization.
ISO 8583
The international messaging standard used for financial transaction card interchange. The wire format that processors use to communicate authorization requests and responses between banks and networks.
ISO (Independent Sales Organization)
A company or individual registered with Visa and/or Mastercard to resell payment processing services under contract from an acquiring bank or processor. ISOs sign up merchants, handle onboarding and support, and earn a residual — a percentage of the processing fees that merchant generates — for the lifetime of the merchant relationship. The ISO does not hold financial liability; the sponsoring acquirer does. Large ISOs manage merchant portfolios worth tens of millions in annual recurring revenue.
ISV (Independent Software Vendor)
A software company that builds and sells applications for a specific industry vertical (e.g., Toast for restaurants, Mindbody for fitness, ServiceTitan for home services). In the payments context, ISVs are significant because they embed payment processing directly into their software, creating a distribution channel for payment services that bypasses traditional ISO channels. The ISV owns the merchant relationship; payments monetization follows naturally from software adoption.
Issuing bank / issuer
The financial institution that issues a card to a cardholder. Responsible for approving or declining transactions, setting credit limits, and handling disputes. Earns interchange on every transaction.
KYC (Know Your Customer)
The identity verification process that financial institutions and PayFacs must perform when onboarding a new customer or merchant. In traditional bank merchant onboarding, KYC involves document review, credit checks, and manual approval — a process taking 1–4 weeks. PayFacs have automated KYC using risk models that approve most merchants in seconds, which is a primary reason PayFac onboarding is faster than traditional merchant account applications.
MCC (Merchant Category Code)
A four-digit code assigned to every merchant that classifies the type of business. Used by issuers to determine interchange rates, apply rewards, and flag unusual spending patterns.
MID (Merchant ID)
A unique identifier assigned by an acquirer to a merchant. The merchant's "account number" with the payment system. Each location, channel, or division may have a separate MID.
MDES (Mastercard Digital Enablement Service)
Mastercard's Token Service Provider platform. MDES creates and manages device tokens (DPANs) for Mastercard cards provisioned to digital wallets (Apple Pay, Google Pay, Samsung Pay) and manages network tokens for merchant card-on-file tokenization. MDES is the Mastercard equivalent of Visa's VTS (Visa Token Service).
NFC (Near-Field Communication)
A short-range wireless technology (range: ~4cm) that enables contactless payments. When a cardholder taps their phone or contactless card on a terminal, NFC is the communication standard that transmits the payment credential. Apple Pay, Google Pay, and contactless EMV chip cards all use NFC. NFC-enabled card-present transactions generate the same low interchange rates as chip-insert transactions.
Network token
A token issued by a card network's Token Service Provider (Visa VTS, Mastercard MDES) that replaces the PAN for a specific merchant. Updated automatically when the underlying card changes.
PAN (Primary Account Number)
The actual card number — 15–19 digits printed on a credit or debit card. Highly sensitive; tokenized or encrypted in virtually every modern payment flow.
PAR (Payment Account Reference)
A 29-character alphanumeric identifier standardized by EMVCo that links all tokens derived from the same PAN. Returned in authorization responses and used by merchants for identity linking across payment methods.
PayFac (Payment Facilitator)
An entity that holds a master MID and onboards merchants as sub-merchants. Enables near-instant merchant onboarding by absorbing the underwriting risk. Square, Stripe, and Toast are PayFacs.
PCI-DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard)
The security standard that governs how cardholder data must be stored, processed, and transmitted. Maintained by the PCI Security Standards Council. Non-compliance results in fines and potential loss of processing rights.
PSP (Payment Service Provider)
A broad term for a company that provides payment processing services, often combining gateway and processor functions. Stripe, Adyen, and Braintree are PSPs.
POS (Point of Sale)
The physical or digital location where a transaction is completed. A POS system includes the hardware (terminal, card reader, display) and software (inventory, order management, receipts) used to process sales. Modern cloud-based POS systems like Toast, Clover, and Lightspeed have replaced legacy on-premise systems across retail and restaurants. "Card-present" transactions are those occurring at a POS terminal where the physical card is present.
SaaS (Software as a Service)
A software delivery model where applications are hosted in the cloud and accessed via subscription rather than installed locally. In the payments context, SaaS is significant because vertical SaaS platforms (Toast, Mindbody, ServiceTitan) own deeply embedded relationships with their merchant customers — making them the dominant distribution channel for embedded payments and financial services.
Residual
The recurring commission an ISO earns on the payment processing fees of the merchants they signed up. Typically expressed in basis points (e.g., "50 bps") and paid monthly for the life of the merchant relationship.
SCA (Strong Customer Authentication)
A regulatory requirement under the EU's PSD2 (Payment Services Directive 2) mandating multi-factor authentication for electronic payments in Europe. SCA requires two of three factors: something the cardholder knows (PIN/password), has (device), or is (biometric). 3D Secure 2.x is the primary technical mechanism used to satisfy SCA for card-not-present transactions. Transactions failing to meet SCA requirements can be declined by the issuer.
SMB (Small and Medium Business)
A merchant classification used loosely across the payments industry, generally referring to businesses with fewer than 500 employees or under $10M–$50M in annual revenue. SMB payments are characterized by a preference for simplicity, fast onboarding, and flat-rate pricing. Square, Stripe, and PayPal built their initial growth on SMB merchant acquisition.
Settlement
The actual movement of funds between banks following a batch of authorized transactions. Happens overnight via ACH or same-day via faster payment rails. The merchant receives funds minus all fees.
Tokenization
The process of replacing sensitive card data (PAN) with a non-sensitive placeholder (token) that has no exploitable value outside the specific system that issued it.
TSP (Token Service Provider)
An entity authorized by a card network to issue and manage payment tokens. For Visa, the TSP is Visa Token Service (VTS). For Mastercard, it is MDES. TSPs provision device tokens (DPANs) when a cardholder adds a card to Apple Pay or Google Pay, and provision network tokens when merchants request card-on-file tokenization. The TSP maintains the mapping between a token and the underlying PAN, and assigns the PAR that links all tokens from the same account.
VTS (Visa Token Service)
Visa's Token Service Provider platform. VTS creates and manages device tokens (DPANs) for Visa cards provisioned to digital wallets and merchant network tokens for card-on-file use. VTS began including PAR in authorization responses in 2019, enabling merchants to link transactions across payment instruments without exposing the underlying PAN. The Mastercard equivalent is MDES.
